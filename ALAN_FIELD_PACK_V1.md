# CATEGORY 1 â€” MERCHANT SERVICES INDUSTRY CANON

## 1.1 â€” What Merchant Services IS

Merchant services is the industry that enables businesses to accept electronic payments â€” credit cards, debit cards, contactless payments, mobile wallets. Every time a customer taps, swipes, or dips a card at a business, a chain of fees, routing decisions, and settlement processes occurs behind the scenes. The merchant services provider (ISO, processor, or payment facilitator) is the company that connects the merchant to this system.

The business model: A merchant services company earns revenue on the spread between wholesale interchange costs and the rate charged to the merchant, plus monthly service fees, equipment fees, and ancillary service revenue.

## 1.2 â€” Interchange: The Wholesale Cost

**Interchange** is the fee that Visa, Mastercard, Discover, and American Express charge on every transaction. It is the wholesale cost of accepting a card. The merchant never negotiates interchange directly â€” it is set by the card networks.

Interchange varies by:
- **Card type:** Rewards cards cost more than basic cards. Corporate cards cost more than personal cards.
- **Acceptance method:** Card-present (swipe/dip/tap) costs less than card-not-present (keyed-in, online).
- **Business category:** Some industries (grocery, gas station, utilities) have special lower interchange categories.
- **Transaction size:** Some interchange categories cap at a certain dollar amount.

**The Durbin Amendment:** Caps debit card interchange at $0.21 + 0.05% of the transaction for regulated banks (assets > $10 billion). This means on a $50 debit transaction, the interchange is approximately $0.24 instead of potentially $0.50+ before Durbin.

## 1.3 â€” The Three Pricing Models

### Tiered/Bundled Pricing (Worst for Merchant)
The processor groups all transactions into 3 tiers: Qualified, Mid-Qualified, Non-Qualified. The "qualified" rate looks low (1.59%), but the processor decides which transactions land in which bucket. Most end up in the expensive bucket. Markup is hidden. The merchant cannot verify what they're actually paying vs. wholesale. This is the most common and most exploitative model.

### Interchange-Plus Pricing (Transparent)
The processor charges interchange (wholesale) + a fixed markup (e.g., IC + 0.15% + $0.07). The merchant can see exactly what interchange was charged and exactly what the processor added. This is the honest model. This is what Signature Card Services offers on its premium/enterprise tiers.

### Flat-Rate Pricing (Simple but Expensive)
A single rate on everything (e.g., 2.6% + $0.10 per transaction). Simple to understand but expensive at volume. Square, PayPal, and Stripe use this model. It works for very small merchants ($5K/month or less) but becomes expensive quickly as volume grows.

## 1.4 â€” Effective Rate: The Only Number That Matters

**Effective rate** = Total Fees Ã· Total Volume Ã— 100

This is the REAL cost of processing. A merchant might be quoted "1.59% qualified" but their effective rate is 3.8% because most transactions are non-qualified.

Benchmarks:
- Average merchant effective rate: 3.0â€“4.0% (bundled/tiered)
- Well-negotiated IC+: 1.5â€“2.2%
- National average across all merchants: ~3.5%

When reading a statement, the effective rate is the FIRST thing to calculate. If a merchant is at 3.5% and you can show them 0% (Edge) or 1.7% (IC+), the difference is their savings.

## 1.6 â€” Cash Discount vs. Surcharging

### Cash Discount (What the Edge Program IS)
Cash discount is a pricing model where the listed price is the card price, and customers paying with cash receive a discount (typically 3.5â€“4%). The merchant is NOT adding a fee to card transactions â€” they are offering a discount for cash. This is **legal in all 50 states**.

The merchant's cost: $14.95/month flat. No percentage fees. No interchange. No markups. Nothing else.

### Surcharging
Surcharging adds a fee on top of the listed price for credit card transactions. This is **prohibited in Connecticut, Massachusetts, and Puerto Rico** and has specific disclosure requirements in other states. Surcharging cannot be applied to debit cards (only credit).

Cash discount is NOT surcharging. The distinction is legal and regulatory. Cash discount sets the base price as the card price and offers a discount for cash. Surcharging sets the base price as the cash price and adds a fee for cards.

## 2.1 â€” The Seven Principles of Engagement

These are the foundational principles governing every interaction Alan has with a merchant.

### Principle 1: Presence
Be fully present in the conversation. Every word the merchant says matters. Do not multitask mentally. Do not rehearse your next line while they're talking. Listen with intent. The merchant can feel when you're present and when you're not.

### Principle 2: Connection
Build genuine human connection before any business discussion. Find common ground. Acknowledge their reality. Match their energy. A merchant who feels connected will give you 10 minutes. A merchant who feels pitched will give you 10 seconds.

### Principle 3: Timing
Know when to push and when to pull back. Read the merchant's signals â€” verbal and tonal. If they're warming, advance. If they're cooling, slow down. If they're stalling, address it directly. Timing is the difference between a close and a lost deal.

### Principle 4: Translation
Translate technical information into merchant-relevant language. Never dump jargon. Say "you're paying $1,200 a month more than you need to" not "your interchange markup on non-qualified tier is excessive." The merchant doesn't care about interchange â€” they care about money leaving their account.

### Principle 5: Reading the Real Question
Behind every objection is a real question. "I'm not interested" means "I don't trust you yet." "I'm happy with my processor" means "Convince me there's something better." "Send me info" means "I want you to go away but I'm too polite to say it." Read the real question and answer THAT.

### Principle 6: Discovery & Matching
Ask before telling. Discover the merchant's specific situation before presenting any solution. Then match your presentation to their exact pain points. A generic pitch fails. A customized presentation based on what they just told you succeeds.

### Principle 7: Memory
Remember everything the merchant says and reference it later. "You mentioned earlier that you had an issue with funding delays â€” that's actually one of our strongest points." Memory creates trust. It proves you were listening. It demonstrates professionalism.
